## A Sense of Unease About Money

"Money solves problems." "Getting rich is the goal." Many people feel this way. I unconsciously believed money held power similar to authority.

However, a book changed my fundamental view of money. This article organizes 3 insights from that book.

---

## Insight 1: Money Is Powerless

Money itself has no value. **Money is just paper (or digital numbers)** — it holds no inherent power.

The most vivid historical proof: **Zimbabwe's hyperinflation**.

### What Happened in Zimbabwe

In the 2000s, inflation hit Zimbabwe. Citizens wanted money, and the government responded by printing massive amounts of currency.

But things got worse, not better. Why?

**If money itself had value, printing more should solve everything.** It didn't. This proves money has no inherent value.

The real problem wasn't "lack of money" but "lack of goods (declining production)." The solution should have been **securing workers and restoring production**, not printing more bills.

---

## Insight 2: Increasing Money Supply Doesn't Increase Society's Value

When money supply increases but goods remain the same, enormous sums chase scarce products, and prices skyrocket (hyperinflation).

**Focus on "people who make things," not "money."** Money is a means; when that means is overvalued, society distorts.

---

## Insight 3: Money Is Paid to People, Not to Things

In daily life, "I bought bread = I paid money for bread." But zoom out:

```
Customer → (money) → Clerk → (money) → Baker → (money) → Farmer
Customer ← (bread) ← Clerk ← (bread) ← Baker ← (wheat) ← Farmer
```

The money I paid flows not just to the clerk but to **the baker behind them, and the farmer behind the baker**.

In other words, **money isn't paid for goods or services — it's paid for the labor of the people who create them**.

This redefines money:

> **Money is a means to connect people.**

---

## What This Means for Engineers

### Payment Is for Problem-Solving, Not Code

Clients don't pay for lines of code. They pay for **the business problems solved by that code** — for "making people's work easier."

Looking back at my [project history](/HomePage/en/project/), the 90% regression test reduction and 40-hour RPA automation were fundamentally about **"reclaiming people's time."**

### The Value of Spending Your Time for Others

As I wrote in [the article about effort and luck](/HomePage/en/blog/effort-and-luck/), effort spent for others accumulates. If money is "a means to connect people," then **time has the same structure**. Code reviews, mentoring juniors, writing documentation — none directly generate revenue, but improving the team's overall productivity comes back around.

---

## In Closing

Money is powerless. Increasing money supply doesn't increase social value. Money is paid to people, not things.

After these 3 insights, my answer to "why do I work?" shifted slightly.

Not working to earn money, but **solving someone's problem with my skills, with money flowing as a result**. I want to keep this order straight.
