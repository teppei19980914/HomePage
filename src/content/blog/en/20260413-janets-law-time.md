## It Started with a Senior's Remark

"As you get older, a year flies by — you age as naturally as breathing."

A senior colleague said this casually at a company event, and it stuck with me. At 27, I don't fully feel it yet, but compared to childhood summer vacations, recent years do pass remarkably fast.

This phenomenon has a name: **Janet's Law**.

---

## What Is Janet's Law?

Proposed by 19th-century French philosopher Paul Janet, it states that **"as the ratio of one year to your total life decreases, subjective time feels shorter."**

| Age | Ratio of 1 Year | Perception |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | **1/5** of life | A year feels very long |
| 27 | **1/27** of life | A year passes fairly quickly |
| 50 | **1/50** of life | A year flies by |

This ratio decline is unavoidable. Time passes equally regardless of how we feel.

However, Janet's Law has another dimension: **"When new experiences decrease, brain activity drops, making time feel shorter."** This part is actionable.

---

## Keeping the Brain Active

When new experiences diminish, the brain processes daily life as "known patterns," and fewer events stick in memory. The result: "I did nothing, yet the year is over."

Conversely, **deliberately incorporating new experiences can stretch subjective time**.

What I practice:

- **Touching unfamiliar technology** — At work I use .NET / SQL Server, but in personal projects I dive into Flutter and Astro. [Building an app in 3 weeks with AI-driven development](/HomePage/en/blog/20260410-ai-driven-development/) was a genuine "brain activation" experience
- **Forcing learning cycles through certifications** — Building up from Fundamental IT Engineer to Applied IT Engineer, LPIC, OSS-DB, and Python certifications. Exam prep isn't "reviewing what I know" but "systematically learning unknown domains"
- **Making output a habit** — Technical articles on Qiita, reflections on this blog. Input alone doesn't activate the brain. The process of articulating thoughts externally reveals new perspectives
- **Never stopping personal development** — [YumeHashi](/HomePage/en/product/yumehashi/) / [Defrago](/HomePage/en/product/defrago/) / this homepage. The act of building continuously generates new challenges

---

## Time Is the Most Valuable Asset

My senior's words led me to realize: **time is more valuable than cars, houses, or money**.

Because:

- **It can't be recovered** — Lost money can be re-earned, but past time never returns
- **It can't be controlled** — No one knows when it ends
- **It's given equally** — Everyone has the same 24 hours

That's why I believe we must **focus on what we consider most important** within the time we're given.

---

## "Live in the Present" — But Specifically

"Live in the present" is correct but insufficient to act on.

I've made it concrete:

- **Write code or read technical articles every day** — Never let a day be zero
- **Articulate learnings as output** — Prevent the brain from entering "known pattern" mode
- **Challenge unfamiliar technology at least once a year** — Force-feed "new experiences" to the brain
- **Regularly review short/mid/long-term goals** — A compass to prevent drifting through time

Time can't be stopped. So I keep striving within it. That's the conclusion I reached from my senior's single remark.
